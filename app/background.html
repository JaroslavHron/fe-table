Periodic Table of the Finite Elements

Background

The table presents the primary spaces of finite elements for the discretization of the fundamental operators of vector calculus: the gradient, curl, and divergence. A finite element space is a space of piecewise polynomial functions on a domain determined by: (1) a mesh of the domain into polyhedral cells called \emph{elements,} (2) a finite dimensional space of polynomial functions on each element called the \emph{shape functions,} and (3) a unisolvent set of functionals on the shape functions of each element called \emph{degrees of freedom} (DOFs), each DOF being associated to a (generalized) face of the element, and specifying a quantity which takes a single value for all elements sharing the face. The element diagrams depict the DOFs and their association to faces.

The spaces $\P_r^-\Lambda^k$ and $\P_r\Lambda^k$ depicted on the left half of the table are the two primary families of finite element spaces for meshes of simplices, and the spaces $\Q_r^-\Lambda^k$ and $\S_r\Lambda^k$ on the right side are for meshes of cubes or boxes. Each is defined in any dimension $n\ge 1$, for each value of the polynomial degree $r\ge 1$, and each value of $0 \leq k \leq n$. The parameter $k$ refers to the operator: the spaces belong to the domain of the $k$th exterior derivative. Thus for $k=0$, the spaces discretize the Sobolev space $H^1$, the domain of the gradient operator; for $k=1$, they discretize $H(\curl)$, the domain of the curl; for $k=n-1$ they discretize $H(\div)$, the domain of the divergence; and for $k=n$, they discretize $L^2$.

The spaces $\P_r^-\Lambda^0$ and $\P_r\Lambda^0$, which coincide, are the earliest finite elements, going back in the case $r=1$ of linear elements to Courant,\endnote{Bull.~Amer.~Math.~Soc.~49, 1943.} and collectively referred to as the Lagrange elements. The spaces $\P_{r+1}^-\Lambda^n$ and $\P_r\Lambda^n$, which also coincide, are the discontinuous Galerkin elements, consisting of piecewise polynomials with no interelement continuity imposed, first introduced by Reed and Hill.\endnote{Los Alamos report~LA-UR-73-479, 1973.} The space $\P_r^-\Lambda^1$ in $2$ dimensions was introduced by Raviart and Thomas\endnote{Lect.~Notes~Math. 606, 1977.} and generalized to the $3$-dimensional spaces $\P_r^-\Lambda^1$ and $\P_r^-\Lambda^2$ by Ned\'el\'ec,\endnote{Numer.~Math.~35, 1980.} while $\P_r\Lambda^1$ is due to Brezzi, Douglas, and Marini\endnote{Numer.~Math.~47, 1985.} in $2$ dimensions, its generalization to $3$ dimensions again due to Ned\'el\'ec.\endnote{Numer.~Math.~50, 1986.} The unified treatment and notation of the $\P_r^-\Lambda^k$ and $\P_r\Lambda^k$ families is due to Arnold, Falk, and Winther as part of finite element exterior calculus.\endnote{Acta~Numerica ~15, 2006.} The space $\P_1^-\Lambda^k$ is the span of the ``elementary forms'' introduced by Whitney.\endnote{\emph{Geometric Integration Theory}, 1957.}

The family $\Q_r^-\Lambda^k$ of cubical elements can be derived from the $1$-dimensional Lagrange and discontinuous Galerkin elements by a tensor product construction detailed by Arnold, Boffi, and Bonizzoni,\endnote{Numer.~Math., 2014.} but for the most part were presented individually along with the corresponding simplicial elements in the papers mentioned. The second cubical family $\S_r\Lambda^k$ is due to Arnold and Awanou.\endnote{Math.~Comp., 2013.}

The finite element spaces on simplices have been implemented as part of the FEniCS Project\endnote{\tt http://fenicsproject.org} and may be accessed in FEniCS via {\tt FunctionSpace(mesh, "P- Lambda", r, k)} and and {\tt FunctionSpace(mesh, "P Lambda", r, k)} for $\P_r^-\Lambda^k$ and $\P_r\Lambda^k$, respectively.\endnote{Automated Solution of Differential Equations by the Finite Element Method, Springer 2012.}

The $\P_r^-\Lambda^k$ family

The shape function space for $\P_r^-\Lambda^k$ is $\P_{r-1}\Lambda^k+\kappa\P_{r-1}\Lambda^{k+1}$ where $\kappa$ is the Koszul differential.$^7$ It includes the full polynomial space $\P_{r-1}\Lambda^k$, is included in $\P_r\Lambda^k$, and has dimension

$$\dim \P_r^-\Lambda^k(\Delta_n)=\binom{r+n}{r+k}\binom{r+k-1}{k}.$$

The degrees of freedom are given on faces $f$ of dimension $d\ge k$ by moments of the trace weighted by a full polynomial space:

$$u\mapsto \int_f (\tr_f u)\wedge q, \quad q\in\P_{r+k-d-1}\Lambda^{d-k}(f).$$

The spaces with constant degree $r$ form a complex:

$$\P_r^-\Lambda^0\xrightarrow{\mathrm d}\P_r^-\Lambda^1\xrightarrow{\mathrm d}\cdots\xrightarrow{\mathrm d}\P_r^-\Lambda^n.$$

The $\P_r\Lambda^k$ family

The shape function space for $\P_r\Lambda^k$ consists of all differential $k$-forms with polynomial coefficients of degree at most $r$, and has dimension

$$\dim \P_r\Lambda^k(\Delta_n)=\binom{r+n}{r+k}\binom{r+k}{k}.$$

The degrees of freedom are given on faces $f$ of dimension $d\ge k$ by moments of the trace weighted by a $\P_r^-$ space:

$$u\mapsto \int_f (\tr_f u)\wedge q, \quad q\in\P^-_{r+k-d}\Lambda^{d-k}(f).$$

The spaces with decreasing degree $r$ form a complex:

$$\P_r\Lambda^0\xrightarrow{\mathrm d}\P_{r-1}\Lambda^1\xrightarrow{\mathrm d}\cdots\xrightarrow{\mathrm d}\P_{r-n}\Lambda^n.$$

The $\Q_r^-\Lambda^k$ family

This family is constructed from the complex of $1$-dimensional finite elements using a tensor product construction.$^{10}$ The shape function space on the unit cube $\square_n=I^n$ is given by

$$\Q_r^-\Lambda^k(\square_n) = \bigoplus_{\sigma\in \Sigma(k,n)}\left[\bigotimes_{i=1}^n \P_{r-\delta_{i,\sigma}}(I)\right]\, \mathrm{d}x^{\sigma_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge \mathrm{d}x^{\sigma_k}, $$

where $\Sigma(k,n)$ denotes the increasing maps $\{1,\ldots,k\}\to\{1,\ldots,n\}$. Its dimension is $\dim \Q_r^-\Lambda^k(\square_n) =\binom{n}{k}r^k(r+1)^{n-k}. $ The degrees of freedom are

$$u \mapsto \int_f (\tr_f u)\wedge q, \quad q\in \Q_{r-1}^-\Lambda^{d-k}(f).$$

The spaces with constant degree $r$ form a complex:

$$\Q_r^-\Lambda^0 \xrightarrow{\mathrm d} \Q_r^-\Lambda^1 \xrightarrow{\mathrm d} \cdots \xrightarrow{\mathrm d} \Q_r^-\Lambda^n.$$

The $\S_r\Lambda^k$ family

The shape function space for $\S_r\Lambda^k$ is given by

$$\P_r\Lambda^k \oplus \bigoplus_{\ell\ge 1}[\kappa\H_{r+\ell-1,\ell}\Lambda^{k+1} \oplus \mathrm{d}\kappa\H_{r+\ell,\ell}\Lambda^k], $$

where $\H_{r,l}\Lambda^k$ consists of homogeneous polynomial $k$-forms of degree $r$ which are linear and undifferentiated in at least $\ell$ variables.$^{11}$ Its dimension is $\dim \S_r\Lambda^k(\square_n) = \sum_{d\ge k} 2^{n-d}\binom{n}{d} \binom{r-d+2k}{d}\binom{d}{k}.$ The degrees of freedom are

$$u\mapsto \int_f (\operatorname{tr}_f u)\wedge q, \quad q\in \P_{r-2(d-k)}\Lambda^{d-k}(f).$$

The spaces with decreasing degree $r$ form a complex:

$$ \S_r\Lambda^0 \xrightarrow{\mathrm d} \S_{r-1}\Lambda^1\xrightarrow{\mathrm d} \cdots \xrightarrow{\mathrm d} \S_{r-n}\Lambda^n.$$
